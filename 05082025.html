Weekly Summary

Sovereign‑debt news during the 29 July–4 August window was dominated by programme adjustments and fiscal resilience measures. Zambia requested a one‑year extension of its US$1.7 billion IMF programme and received a US$184 million disbursement after a broadly satisfactory fifth review, highlighting lingering debt‑distress risks and the need to finalise deals with remaining creditors. Argentina saw its US$20 billion extended fund arrangement advance after the IMF board completed the first review, but subsequent updates revealed that net‑reserve targets were eased due to shortfalls, signalling ongoing external‑liquidity pressures. Smaller economies also featured prominently: Sao Tomé & Príncipe and Mali underwent IMF reviews stressing debt sustainability, governance, and climate resilience. World Bank operations in El Salvador and Mongolia underscored the emergence of climate‑contingent loans and the need to broaden revenue bases. Sri Lanka, Senegal and the Philippines highlighted domestic resource mobilisation and transparency issues.

Zambia seeks 12‑month extension of IMF Extended Credit Facility amid unfinished debt talks

Zambia — 30-07-2025 — Reuters
Zambia’s government asked the IMF to extend its 38‑month Extended Credit Facility (ECF) by another year beyond its scheduled October 2025 expiry. The US$1.7 billion programme was approved in 2022 and has disbursed roughly US$1.55 billion. Officials argue an extension is necessary to consolidate reforms, tackle inflation and finalise debt‑restructuring agreements. The country defaulted on its US$18 billion external debt in 2020 and has reached a restructuring deal with major creditors but still needs to conclude agreements with smaller lenders such as Afreximbank. The extension proposal signals that Zambia remains at high risk of debt distress despite improvements, and negotiations with private creditors will determine whether the debt treatment remains comparable across creditors. IMF staff are expected to present the request to the Executive Board later this year.

Zambia — 25-07-2025 — International Monetary Fund

The review noted broadly satisfactory performance against quantitative targets but stressed that Zambia remains at high risk of debt distress. Officials emphasised the need to finalise restructuring agreements with all official and private creditors and maintain a primary surplus through revenue mobilisation and prudent spending. The programme aims to restore fiscal sustainability and achieve a medium‑term reduction in external debt distress. IMF directors called for continued public financial management reforms and improved debt transparency as the country seeks to shift from high to moderate risk of external debt distress by the mid‑2020s.
