Weekly Summary

During the week from 16 to 22 August 2025, sovereign‑debt markets were shaped by rating actions, ongoing restructurings and innovative financing mechanisms. Fitch affirmed several sovereign‑linked entities – including Germany’s KfW, Romania, New Zealand and Iceland – highlighting strong policy frameworks but warning of high twin deficits, delayed consolidation and external vulnerabilities. S&P reaffirmed the US ‘AA+’ rating, noting that tariff revenues could offset deficits as national debt exceeds US$37 trillion. Africa remained in focus: Ghana paid US$708.1 million in coupons under its domestic debt exchange, secured a draft US$5.4 billion bilateral restructuring, and saw its domestic swap saving 61 billion cedi and boosting growth. Zambia continued implementing its G20 Common Framework deal, but low tax revenues and governance issues impede progress. Kenya explored buying back short‑term local bonds to ease looming maturities, while Cameroon authorised CFA 930 billion in borrowing despite IMF warnings. Fitch’s analysis of Mexican banks assessed a new trust fund to support PEMEX as credit‑neutral, and commentary on an Armenia–Azerbaijan peace framework noted potential credit benefits but cautioned timelines. Overall, rating agencies emphasised credible fiscal anchors, but high debt burdens and complex restructurings continue to challenge sovereigns.

1. Fitch analysis: Mexican government’s PEMEX support uses trust fund, credit‑neutral for banks

Fitch Ratings said the Mexican government’s plan to ease Petróleos Mexicanos’ (PEMEX) financial pressure is credit‑neutral for commercial banks. A new trust fund called Eagle Funding issued US$12 billion of pre‑capitalised notes to refinance expensive debt, and a MXN 250 billion (about US$13 billion) investment vehicle financed by commercial banks, development banks and the pension fund AFORE will underpin a long‑term capital injection. Fitch notes that the seven largest Mexican banks’ exposure to the public sector is about 26 % of assets and that the trust fund reduces refinancing risk without increasing credit risk. By shifting obligations off banks’ balance sheets and extending maturities, the scheme stabilises PEMEX’s debt while preserving bank capital. Further monitoring will assess the trust’s performance and any additional government guarantees.

2. Romania’s ‘BBB‑’ rating affirmed; negative outlook reflects fiscal deterioration

Fitch affirmed Romania’s long‑term foreign‑currency issuer rating at ‘BBB‑’ and kept the outlook negative. The agency said the rating reflects EU membership and strong capital inflows but is constrained by weak public finances, large fiscal deficits and rising debt‑to‑GDP ratios. Fitch projects the budget deficit to remain about 4.7 % of GDP in 2025 and warns that political polarisation and delays in fiscal consolidation undermine confidence. Public debt is expected to continue rising from a moderate base, and the negative outlook signals that further slippage could trigger a downgrade. Next milestones include the government’s ability to pass reforms under EU fiscal rules and to curb primary deficits.

3. Zambia restructures US$6.3 billion of bilateral debt under G20 Common Framework

Zambia defaulted on its debt in 2020 when public liabilities reached 103.5 % of GDP and has since negotiated a restructuring of US$6.3 billion in bilateral debt under the G20 Common Framework. The deal stretches maturities to 2043 and fixes interest at 1 % until 2037, offering substantial cash‑flow relief. While the agreement covers official creditors, private bondholders – who hold a significant share of Zambia’s external debt – have not yet signed comparable terms. Fitch notes that Zambia’s tax‑to‑GDP ratio remains low (16.8 %), and governance challenges persist, limiting revenue mobilisation. The IMF’s next review will assess whether fiscal reforms and diversification strategies (such as agriculture) can support debt sustainability. Securing private‑creditor participation and improving tax collection are key milestones.

4. Ghana’s domestic debt exchange saves 61 billion cedi and supports economic recovery

FurtherAfrica reports that Ghana’s domestic debt exchange programme, completed in late 2023, saved around 61 billion cedi and paved the way for a modest economic rebound. The swap involved converting high‑coupon domestic bonds into longer‑maturity instruments with lower yields, easing cash‑flow pressures and helping Ghana achieve 5.3 % year‑on‑year GDP growth in the first quarter of 2025. The article notes that Ghana has secured a draft US$5.4 billion bilateral debt restructuring under the G20 Common Framework, but a dispute with Afreximbank over a US$750 million loan complicates progress. Legislative reforms and penalties for extra‑budgetary spending have restored some market confidence, yet the country still faces high inflation and currency volatility. Next steps include finalising agreements with commercial creditors and implementing fiscal reforms under the IMF programme.

5. United States retains ‘AA+’ rating as tariff revenue seen offsetting fiscal deficits

S&P Global Ratings reaffirmed the United States’ long‑term sovereign rating at ‘AA+’ with a stable outlook, noting that revenue from President Donald Trump’s tariffs could help offset the fiscal impact of a US$3.3 trillion tax‑and‑spending bill. Primary analyst Lisa Schineller said future rating actions will hinge on how budget execution and tariff receipts affect growth and deficits. Analysts estimate the bill could add US$3.3 trillion to the national debt over the next decade, while U.S. national debt has already surpassed US$37 trillion and the effective tariff rate may reach 18.6 %. S&P cautioned that policy uncertainty remains high and that the benefits of tariffs depend on trade dynamics. The next milestone will be the release of federal budget updates and a review of tariff collection data.

6. Ghana’s draft US$5.4 billion bilateral restructuring under G20 Common Framework faces challenges

The article notes that Ghana has secured a draft agreement with its official creditors to restructure US$5.4 billion of bilateral debt under the G20 Common Framework. The plan would reschedule payments due between December 2022 and December 2026, capitalising arrears and repaying them from 2039 to 2043. Interest rates on rescheduled amounts would range from 1 % to 3 %, substantially below market levels, providing debt‑service relief during Ghana’s IMF‑supported programme. However, private creditors hold about 43 % of Africa’s external debt and delays in achieving comparability of treatment could undermine the deal. The article also criticises the voluntary nature of the Common Framework, arguing that enforcement mechanisms are weak. Upcoming steps include finalising the memorandum of understanding with the official creditor committee and engaging private bondholders.

7. Ghana pays US$708.1 million coupon under Domestic Debt Exchange Programme

Financial Afrik reports that Ghana’s Treasury paid US$708.1 million in interest under its Domestic Debt Exchange Programme on 19 August, marking the fifth coupon payment since the programme began in 2023. The successful payment follows previous delays and is expected to bolster investor confidence as Ghana continues to negotiate external debt relief. The government aims to maintain timely servicing to avoid triggering cross‑default clauses and to build credibility ahead of talks with private creditors. Upcoming milestones include the next semi‑annual coupon in early 2026 and finalising external restructuring terms.

8. Fitch affirms KfW at ‘AAA’; stable outlook backed by German government guarantee

Fitch affirmed German development bank KfW’s long‑term issuer default rating at ‘AAA’ with a stable outlook, highlighting the highest probability of support from the German state. KfW’s ratings are equalised with those of Germany (AAA/Stable/F1+) due to an explicit, direct and unconditional guarantee provided by the federal government and a maintenance obligation ensuring KfW’s continuation as an economic entity. Fitch notes that KfW’s important policy role, strong capitalisation and mandate to finance development projects underpin the rating. The stable outlook reflects expectations that sovereign support will continue and that KfW’s risk‑management practices will remain conservative. Future developments to watch include changes to Germany’s fiscal policy and any amendments to KfW’s guarantee framework.

9. Fitch affirms Erste Abwicklungsanstalt (EAA) at ‘AAA’ due to state guarantee

Fitch affirmed Erste Abwicklungsanstalt’s (EAA) long‑term issuer rating at ‘AAA’ with a stable outlook. The German bad‑bank vehicle’s ratings are equalised with those of the State of North Rhine‑Westphalia (NRW) and Germany because the Financial Market Stabilisation Fund (FMSA) and NRW provide a deficiency guarantee ensuring that EAA can meet its liabilities even if other owners cannot. This guarantee, combined with a maintenance obligation that ensures EAA’s continuation, underpins the top rating. Fitch emphasises that EAA’s mandate is to wind down the assets of the former WestLB and that it is not intended to operate on a commercial basis. The stable outlook reflects continued state support and a gradual reduction of risk exposures. Upcoming milestones include monitoring progress on asset wind‑down and any legislative changes to the guarantee.

10. Fitch affirms New Zealand at ‘AA+’; stable outlook amid high current account deficit

Fitch affirmed New Zealand’s long‑term foreign‑currency rating at ‘AA+’ with a stable outlook, citing a resilient, high‑income economy and strong policy framework but noting vulnerabilities from high current‑account deficits and elevated household debt. Fitch also warns that a sharper‑than‑expected slowdown in China could weaken exports and that inflation remains above the 2 % target. Upcoming developments include the government’s mid‑term fiscal update and monetary policy decisions by the Reserve Bank of New Zealand, which will influence debt dynamics.

Iceland’s rating benefits from strong institutions and high per‑capita income but is constrained by a relatively small, volatile economy and lingering external imbalances. 13. Kenya considers buying back short‑term bonds as maturities loom

Kenya is exploring a buyback of local bonds maturing in 2025 using proceeds from issuing longer‑dated securities, in an effort to smooth its debt maturity profile. The country faces 495 billion Kenyan shillings (about US$3.84 billion) in local bond maturities this year and 822 billion shillings next year. The Treasury says the buyback would ease refinancing pressures and support liquidity management. Kenya previously issued a Eurobond in 2024 to repay a US$2 billion Eurobond maturing in June 2024, demonstrating an appetite for proactive liability management. An International Monetary Fund mission is expected in September to negotiate a new financing programme after the prior Extended Credit Facility expired in April 2025. Upcoming milestones include the September IMF visit and details of the bond‑buyback plan.

14. Cameroon authorises CFA 930 billion borrowing to fund projects and clear arrears

Cameroon’s parliament authorised up to 930 billion CFA francs (US$1.67 billion) in new borrowing to finance infrastructure projects and clear domestic arrears, comprising 350 billion CFA in domestic treasury bonds, 250 billion CFA from domestic private lenders and 330 billion CFA from international banks. The measure comes despite the IMF’s assessment that Cameroon is at high risk of debt distress, although its debt remains sustainable at about 43 % of GDP. Finance Minister Louis Paul Motaze said the borrowing will support energy, agriculture and transport projects, while analysts warned that persistent poor management and opaque spending could exacerbate vulnerabilities. The IMF will monitor implementation, and future milestones include publication of the borrowing calendar and progress on arrears clearance.

16. Ghanaian banks’ capital adequacy improves but six banks may need more support

A Fitch Solutions report summarised by the Ghanaian Times warns that six Ghanaian banks face capital compliance challenges following the Domestic Debt Exchange Programme. Two banks remain undercapitalised despite receiving government support, and additional injections may be required before the end of 2025. The Bank of Ghana lowered the capital adequacy requirement from 13 % to 10 % and allowed losses on cedi bonds to be recognised over four years, which, together with high‑yield treasury investments and an appreciating currency, has helped the average capital adequacy ratio rise from 8.7 % in February 2024 to 18.2 % by mid‑2025. However, losses on Eurobond holdings must be recognised immediately, making foreign‑exchange exposure a key risk. Future milestones include the expiry of regulatory forbearance at year‑end and the completion of external debt restructuring.

17. Fitch commentary: Armenia–Azerbaijan peace framework may support credit trends

Fitch Ratings noted that a peace framework between Armenia and Azerbaijan could be credit‑positive but is unlikely to impact ratings immediately. Sustained peace would reduce geopolitical risk, ease foreign‑exchange volatility and allow both countries to redirect resources from defence spending to growth‑enhancing investment. Fitch stresses that benefits would materialise over several years and depend on successful implementation of the agreement, which remains uncertain amid lingering tensions. The agency also points out that Armenia’s defence spending is projected to rise in the short term and that durable risk reduction is needed before ratings could be adjusted. Next milestones include details of the peace agreement and monitoring political developments.
